Zebra Mussel

Dreissena polymorpha

"Zebra mussel."
Retrieved 14 Jun 2019 from https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Dreissena_polymorpha3.jpg/220px-Dreissena_polymorpha3.jpg.

Classification

Phylum: Mollusca
Class: Bivalvia
Order: Veneroida*
Family: Dreissenidae
Genus: Dreissena
Species: polymorpha

Description

Size: about 15 mm on average. The maximum length is around 51 mm (~2 inches)
Coloration: colour patterns vary; they generally have dark zigzag stripes on their lighter-coloured shell

Habitat

Native to: Caspian Sea, Black Sea
Introduced to: North America, United Kingdom, Italy, Spain, Sweden
Found on: substrates around water, including but not limited to docks, boats, pipes, buoys and even other mussels

Feeding

The zebra mussel is a filter feeder; it filters plankton out from water passing through itself to eat the plankton. It has a set of ctenidia, specialised gills, through which the water is filtered for food.

Digestion

A rod called a crystalline style is ground against a hard region of the stomach wall known as the gastric shield; doing so releases enzymes that aid with digesting the food. These are brought into separate sections called diverticula, where food, now suspended in a liquid, is digested in individual cells.

Excretion

The mussel excretes waste as pellets.

Respiration

Zebra mussels have large gills through which they breathe, albeit needing much less than their gills suggest to provide.

Circulation

Blood is pumped from the heart into two main arteries, entering body cavities called hemocoels to distribute blood locally. Blood flows back to the heart through either the ctenidia or the kidneys.

Reproduction

Males and females separately deposit sperm and eggs into the water. Larvae are fertilised and born in the water, after which they will drift in the water for weeks before settling on a hard substrate.

Trivia

  • The zebra mussel is highly invasive and has caused many problems by outcompeting native species for plantkon and disrupting the balance between visually-inclined predators and their prey by disrupting water clarity in eating plankton.
  • It was accidentally introduced to the US via ballast exchange of a commercial ship. Contamination is not to be underestimated!
  • The zebra mussel is particularly resilient in terms of environmental condition; adults can survive out of water for up to seven days.
  • Other than the inherent ecological problems it causes, it is also a large nuisance to humans due to their tendency to biofoul facilities like hydroelectric plants and public water supply plants by attaching itself on pipes and the like.
