Paedophryne amauensis

"Dwarfed by a dime, Paedophryne amauensis inhabits New Guinean rain forests."

Classification

Phylum: Chordata
Class: Amphibia
Order: Anura
Family: Microhylidae
Genus: Paedophryne
Species: amauensis

Description

Length: 7.7 mm
Coloration: mostly brown, with black spots that resemble generic landmass on a map.

Habitat

Native to: Papua New Guinea
Found in: leaf litter (the layer of dead leaves on the ground) in rain forests

Feeding

It is not wholly clear how it eats, due to its relatively recent discovery and large difficulty even finding and recording it. Since it likely eats small invertebrates like mites, it is not unlikely that it stays still and waits for prey to be close enough to just nab it.

Digestion

P. amauensis has an esophagus, stomach, instestines, and a cloaca. The tract ends through the anus. Food is broken down in the stomach as with most animals with a stomach.

Excretion

Waste exits through the cloaca/anus.

Respiration

Being a frog, P. amauensis breathes via buccal pumping, in which it visibly inflates and deflates part of itself to provide air flow. A buccal cavity expands or contracts; expanding draws air in, while contracting pushes air out. While some of the air that is pushed out goes back into the atmosphere, a sufficient amount reaches the lungs. As an amphibian, its skin also allows for much diffusion of environmental molecules into its body, allowing it to respire in part via this diffusion as well.

Circulation

P. amauensis has three blood circuits: one is the regular circuit throughout the body, one is the regular circuit between the heart and lungs, and the third circuit carries deoxygenated blood near the skin for diffusion of oxygen from the air into the blood.

Reproduction

The male and female concurrently deposit sex cells, so that the female does not actually carry fertilised eggs, but deposits them as they are fertilised. It is possible that the frogs create a small "nest" or other hiding place for breeding, which many frogs that mate terrestrially do. Eggs do not hatch into tadpoles; rather, the frogs are born already resembling the shape of the adults.

Trivia

  • P. amauensis is considered the smallest known vertebrate as a species at 7.7 mm.
  • Parts of P. amauensis ' skeleton remain soft throughout adulthood, whereas corresponding parts in other frogs harden. This is probably how they are so small.
  • P. amauensis has been observed jumping at least 30 times its length.
