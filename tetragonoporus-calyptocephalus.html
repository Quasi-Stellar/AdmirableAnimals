Tetragonoporus calyptocephalus

"Once again, we've reached a scenario I really don't want to try to illustrate. Here's another squirrel." Randall Munroe, xkcd what-if #105.
There are no images of T. calyptocephalus due to the apparently minimal amount of research done on it.

Classification

Phylum: Platyhelminthes
Class: Cestoda
Order: Diphyllobothriidea
Family: Diphyllobothriidae
Genus: Tetragonoporus
Species: calyptocephalus

Description

Length: purportedly up to 40 m (130 ft)
Coloration: white in the front; grey elsewhere

Habitat

Native to: Arctic waters
Found in: the intestines of toothed whales

Feeding

Tapeworms absorb nutrients through their exterior, a hard cuticle called a tegument. There is no exact mouth through which it eats.

Digestion

Since it just absorbs foods from its host, the digestion is essentially done by the whale. They have no digestive tract themselves.

Excretion

Since it doesn't digest anything itself, it presumably doesn't have to excrete waste products.

Respiration

Tapeworms, of which T. calyptocephalus is a species, do not have centralised respiration systems; their teguments allow free gas exchange with the environment.

Circulation

. calyptocephalus, like other tapeworms, lacks a circulatory system.

Reproduction

T. calyptocephalus is hermaphroditic; it carries both male and female reproductive systems. It is therefore able to reproduce independently. The eggs are dispeled from the original host via its excrement.

Trivia

  • T. calyptocephalus is currently the longest known tapeworm.
  • Its genus name, Tetragonoporus, is literally "four gonopore"; a gonopore is a type of pore for reproduction, so the name somewhat refers to its hermaphroditism.
  • It is the only species in its genus.
